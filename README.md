## Abstract
Title: _The Last Sushi in the World_ 
Alternative: _The Sushi of Theseus_

The story of sushi is the story of the world’s globalization. The economic, logistical, and cultural entanglement of nations across the world has transformed a niche cuisine (that is bizarre to most cultures and palettes) into one of the most desired and iconic foodstuffs of the modern age. Sushi has turned tuna — once a junk fish in most countries outside of Japan — into one of the most expensive meats on the planet. However, the ravenous appetite for this cuisine and the uncertain future of the oceans in a climate change future threaten to imperil the whole operation. When will these resources run out - when will the last sushi roll be made? Using data from fisheries/agriculture (population, price, climate change effects), consumer trends (price, consumer tastes), and logistical realities (geography, logistical costs), this thesis will determine the future availability of critical sushi ingredients (fish, rice, seaweed) and discuss local and global alternatives based on historical culinary practices. This thesis will address the current situation and potential futures of sushi from a consumer’s perspective — where do the ingredients you’re eating come from? Will you be able to eat this in the future? — and from a producer’s perspective — what is still considered sushi? What alternatives are available and will consumers be willing to eat them?

## Introduction


The history of sushi is the history of change and adaptation to new conditions. Sushi evolved from older methods of fermenting fish in salt in rice to prevent spoilage, and sushi as we know it today — made of three key ingredients rice, fish, and (sometimes optionally) seaweed — came about in Edo period Japan, as chefs replaced fermented rice with vinegared rice and developed common styles such as nigiri (hand pressed) or maki (hand rolled). From the 1800s to 1900s, sushi evolved from a street/fast food cuisine for the urban Japanese middle class (wikipedia source?) to an extremely popular, restaurant-served cuisine. Refrigeration was key in this process, allowing fresh or frozen fish to be shipped longer distances than before. Growth in Japan was mirrored by growth in the western world, with Sushi becoming extremely popular in 80’s and 90’s America. Sushi has continued to be popular and will likely continue to be so.

The modern sushi trade is supported by a massive and highly global network of fishermen and distributors. Tuna, one of the most popular fish used in sushi, is the fish that best represents this network. The sushi industry has turned tuna into a junk fish caught with little commercial value into one of the most expensive cuts of meat in the world, despite its relatively quick spoilage (Orth 2023). Tuna is so valuable and the margins are so high that a fish caught off the coast of Massachusetts can be flown to Tokyo, priced, and sold to a sushi restaurant in Boston (Feng 2012). Other types of fish, such as salmon, have become popular in modern day sushi, but these other types of fish are either easier to harvest or not popular enough to shape the sushi supply chain the way tuna does. In its early history, sushi was shaped by what fish was seasonally available and local enough that it could be made without spoilage. Sushi in the modern day warps global fishing fleets to its wants and needs.

Heavy demand for tuna has led to the overfishing of much of the world’s tuna. Of the 15 tuna species, three are caught for use in sushi: Bluefin, Yellowtail, and Bigeye. All three are considered to be overfished. Climate change driven phenomena — like the warming, acidification, and general depletion of the world’s oceans — similarly threaten these populations, albeit in complicated or non-linear ways. However, research shows that while many fish stocks remained overexploited, many stocks have been rebuilt and are harvested at sustainable rates (Worm et al. 2009, Hilborn et al. 2020). So too with tuna: with aggressive and strict stock management, some tuna populations have recovered and have reached new population heights (NOAA Fisheries 2023). This muddies the picture somewhat as the oceans are not strictly dying, but rather changing rapidly. The swiftly growing fish-farming/aquaculture — while its long term sustainability and impact on ocean health is unclear — will likely shape the availability and affordability of different fish in the near future.

This paper will combine and analyze the drivers of the international sushi market to determine when the fish used to make sushi will run out, thus answering the question “when will the last sushi in the world be made?” This paper will focus on the major influences on tuna populations (sea temperature warming, harvesting, and management practices), the climate impact and carbon cost of air freight (used in shipping high-grade tuna across the world), and the price and availability of sushi across Japan and the United States. Data will be collected mainly from publicly available NOAA, USDA, and JapanGov datasets detailing import/export, fishing/harvesting, and consumption of tuna species, as well as supplementary datasets outline species distributions and global sea temperature changes. As one of the most farmed fish in the world, salmon will be considered. Other sushi ingredients — specifically rice and seaweed — will be addressed but not explored in depth.
